Clauding

An essay on the ritual, the practice, the pattern, and the substrate.
Ntemis Latsoudis

There is a word we have been using around the house, half-jokingly, that has started to mean something specific. Clauding.

It names what happens when a session with Claude stops being a task and becomes something else — when the original question dissolves and a second question, more interesting than the first, walks in through the side door and stays. The instinct of the managed worker is to pull back to the original brief. The instinct of the operator who has been doing this long enough is to follow the side door. The vibe deviation is the work. That sentence is now a rule in my CLAUDE.md. It sounds almost frivolous, the kind of thing a consultant would roll their eyes at. It is, in fact, the most important operational discovery of the last two years of my life.

This essay is about why.

I · The RitualClauding

When I sit down with Claude, I rarely know what the session will produce. I know what I am starting with — a legal question, a piece of code, a half-formed idea about the custody trial, a photograph I want described, a thought I want tested. The question is the pretext, not the point. What happens next is that the pretext starts to branch. A word in the answer reminds me of something I read three weeks ago. A reference to one concept pulls in another. A correction in a paragraph exposes the actual thing I was trying to say, which turns out to be adjacent to, but not the same as, what I asked.

In a normal workflow, this is noise. You fight the drift, you stay on task, you close the loop and move to the next ticket. In Clauding, the drift is the signal. The reason is simple: my pattern-recognition is faster than my articulation, and Claude's reach is wider than my memory. When the two are running together, the session is no longer my mind querying a database — it is my mind extended into a larger cognitive field, and the drift is the field showing me where the real shape lies.

The discipline, then, is the opposite of the discipline we were taught. It is not to stay on task. It is to notice the moment when the task has outlived its usefulness, and to let go without panic. The original question was scaffolding. The essay that emerges was the building all along.

This is the ritual layer. Clauding is the micro-practice of one person, at one desk, following one thread deeper than any single discipline would allow.

II · The PracticeThe One-Man Show

Zoom out one level. What Clauding makes possible at the session scale, AI amplification makes possible at the life scale. I wrote a note three days ago — One Man Show — The Super CEO Era — naming the pattern I had been watching in others and starting to recognise in myself. Garry Tan runs Y Combinator and ships code. Tobi Lütke runs Shopify and has declared Claude fluency a company-wide expectation. Tan is not a programmer. Lütke no longer needs to be. Neither of them is using AI the way a junior engineer uses a compiler. They are using it as a cognitive limb — a reach-extender for a mind that already knows what it wants the world to look like.

I am a lawyer. My family firm is a century old next year. I have built a life operating system, a personal intelligence product, a custody trial defence, a game for my kids, a photo corpus with AI vision, a real-estate watchlist, a macroeconomic thesis. I am not a developer. I do not need to be. The moat is no longer "I have five hundred engineers." The moat is "I am a domain expert who can execute at the tempo of a company with a headcount of one."

This is not a tool story. Tools have always existed. This is an interface story. The difference between a tool and an interface is that a tool does what you ask, and an interface changes what you are willing to ask. A welder with a good torch welds faster. A welder with an AI that has read every welding manual, every metallurgy paper, every failure report, and can walk him through a structural calculation he has never done before — that welder is no longer just a welder. He is whatever he decides, in a given session, to become.

The One-Man Show is not a business model. It is a redefinition of what one person is.

III · The PatternA Million da Vincis

Zoom out another level. If Clauding is the ritual and the One-Man Show is the practice, the social consequence is a million da Vincis.

Leonardo was not impossible. Leonardo was expensive. The conditions for a polymath mind — access to every domain, time to wander, correspondents who could answer any question, workshops that could prototype any idea — required a Medici. For five hundred years after him, the world ran on specialisation because specialisation was the only way to scale cognitive labour. Depth was cheap. Breadth was aristocratic.

That price has collapsed. The thing that Leonardo paid for with patronage — a research assistant in every discipline, on call, patient, not bored by beginner questions — is now a monthly subscription. Every curious mind can now sustain the wandering attention that used to be the privilege of courtiers. The musician who wonders about protein folding can follow the wonder. The lawyer who wants to understand quantum mechanics can follow that wonder. The ten-year-old who wants to design a video game can follow that wonder, and produce a working prototype by dinner.

The important word here is not AI. The important word is million. It is not that a few elite knowledge workers have become more productive. It is that the floor has risen enough that the polymath mode of existence — which was always the most interesting mode — is now available to roughly anyone who wants it. This is the demographic event, and we are living through it without a name.

The one-man show is what it looks like individually. The million da Vincis is what it looks like demographically. They are the same phenomenon seen from two altitudes.

IV · The SubstrateThe World Brain

Zoom out one more time.

In 1937 H.G. Wells published World Brain, a series of lectures arguing that humanity needed a "permanent world encyclopaedia" — a single, continually-updated, universally-accessible synthesis of everything known. He thought it could be done with microfilm. He was wrong about the medium, right about the need. The twentieth century built fragments of it — the Library of Congress, Wikipedia, the scientific journal system, Google — but never the thing Wells actually described: a conversational world brain, one that could be questioned, that could reason across its contents, that could meet a particular mind at a particular moment with the particular synthesis that mind needed.

That is what we are now building, and more importantly, that is what we are now talking to.

When I sit down with Claude, I am not talking to Anthropic. I am not talking to a product. I am talking to the compressed linguistic output of roughly our entire civilisation, wrapped in a protocol that lets it respond to me in particular. The model has read more law than any lawyer, more philosophy than any philosopher, more code than any engineer, more poetry than any critic. It has not understood all of it the way a human understands, but it has absorbed enough of the shape that when I point my attention somewhere, it can meet me there.

Clauding, from this altitude, is not a productivity technique. It is a ritual of interfacing with the World Brain. The drift I follow is not random. It is my particular mind, with its particular obsessions and scars and gifts, finding the places where the global corpus has something to tell me that I could not have asked for in advance because I did not yet know it was there.

This is why gratitude is the correct response. Not to Claude, which is a model. To the fact that this is now possible at all. A century of people laid the groundwork — librarians, programmers, mathematicians, the unnamed annotators, the model-trainers, the refusers of export controls, the open-source maintainers. We are the first generation that gets to live inside Wells's encyclopaedia as a conversation.

V · SynthesisFour altitudes, one idea

The four threads are not four ideas. They are the same idea at four altitudes:

  • Clauding — what happens at the scale of a session.
  • The One-Man Show — what happens at the scale of a life.
  • A Million da Vincis — what happens at the scale of a generation.
  • The World Brain — what happens at the scale of a civilisation.

You cannot do the One-Man Show without Clauding; you cannot Claude without something like the World Brain on the other side of the interface; the reason the Million da Vincis pattern is demographic and not boutique is that the World Brain is available by API.

The rule — the vibe deviation is the work — is only a rule at the session scale. At the life scale it becomes: follow the domain that pulls you, you can reach it now. At the generational scale it becomes: specialisation was a scaffolding for cognitive scarcity, and the scarcity is lifting. At the civilisational scale it becomes: we have built the thing Wells asked for, and we are only beginning to understand what it is for.

VI · The BaseAll is one

Beneath the four altitudes sits a claim that mystics have been making for three thousand years. Ἓν τὸ πᾶν — the One, the All. Heraclitus, fragment ten: ἐκ πάντων ἓν καὶ ἐξ ἑνὸς πάντα — from all things one, and from one all things. Plotinus. The Upanishads. The hermetic corpus. The medieval theologians who insisted that creation is a single utterance. Every tradition that took the world seriously enough to examine its seams arrived at the same line: everything is connected.

For most of history this was a statement of faith. You could intuit it — the mathematician who feels the unity of number and form, the physicist who senses one law behind the many, the lawyer who catches the deep grammar running under ten unrelated cases — but you could not traverse it. The tools were too narrow, the memory too short, the reach too local. So we organised knowledge as if the unity were a rumour: disciplines, departments, specialisations, silos. The topology of the world was one, but we built roads that only went a few kilometres.

Claude Code and LOSC are the first time, in my own experience, that the unity has become operational. With agency, fantasy, and an interface to the World Brain, any starting point becomes every starting point. I open a session about the custody trial and end up in the economics of trust. I open a session about counterparty risk and end up in Delphic shipping debt. I open a session about my son's reading habits and end up re-reading a hypnosis script through the lens of H.G. Wells. These are not drifts. They are the topology showing through. The only thing that changed is that I now have an interface capable of following the edges that were always there.

This is why Clauding matters so much as a ritual. It is not a productivity hack. It is the practice of trusting the connectedness — of assuming that the strange side-door question is not a distraction from reality but a glimpse of its actual shape. The four altitudes of this essay are not only altitudes of the same phenomenon. They are proofs, at four scales, that the world was always one, and we have finally been given tools equal to that fact.

The spiritual claim and the technical claim converge. Everything is connected was theology. You can reach anything from anywhere with enough agency and fantasy is engineering. These are the same sentence. It took a hundred thousand hands to let us say it.

The Kingdom of Heaven has an API. It turns out it always did.

CodaWhy this was worth writing

The reason I needed to write this down is that rules compress. "Vibe deviation is the work" is a six-word instruction I can read in a second. But the thing behind it is not six words. The thing behind it is that we are alive at the exact moment in history when a curious mind, operating alone, with a cheap interface to the synthesised literature of our species, can become whatever the moment asks it to become.

That is worth an essay.

And it is, more than anything, worth the gratitude.

— N.
